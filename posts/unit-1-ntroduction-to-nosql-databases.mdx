Coming into DBS302, I already had a solid foundation in relational 
databases from DBS101 covering ER diagrams, normalization up to 3NF, 
SQL queries and ACID transactions. I thought I understood databases.

## Why Does NoSQL Even Exist?

Before jumping into what NoSQL *is*, I think it is important to 
understand *why* it was created because it was not born out of 
ideology. It was born out of necessity.

The relational model worked brilliantly from the 1970s through the 
early 2000s. Edgar F. Codd's invention gave us structured and 
predictable data storage with powerful SQL and rock-solid ACID 
guarantees. For online banking, airline reservations and e-commerce 
with thousands of users it was perfect.

Then Web 2.0 hit.

Facebook was storing billions of messages. Twitter was handling 
thousands of tweets per second. Google was indexing the entire web. 
Suddenly relational databases became the bottleneck because they 
scale *vertically* by buying a bigger server. There is a physical 
ceiling to how big one machine can be and that same machine is also 
a single point of failure.

The big tech companies could not wait for Oracle to solve their 
problems. So they built their own systems.

Google built **Bigtable** in 2004, which directly inspired Apache 
HBase and Cassandra. Amazon followed with **Dynamo** in 2007, which 
became the foundation for DynamoDB and Riak. Facebook then 
open-sourced **Cassandra** in 2008, which grew into Apache Cassandra 
as we know it today.

These internal systems sparked an open-source movement. The term 
"NoSQL" itself was coined almost by accident as a hashtag chosen 
for a 2009 San Francisco meetup by Johan Oskarsson. It stuck. And 
while the name suggests "no SQL ever", the community now prefers 
the more accurate interpretation of **Not Only SQL** meaning NoSQL 
*complements* relational databases rather than replacing them.

## So What Actually Is NoSQL?

A NoSQL database is a non-relational database system that stores 
and manages data using flexible schemas and alternative data models 
designed for horizontal scalability and high availability in 
large-scale distributed applications.

The key characteristics that set it apart from what I already knew:

**Schema flexibility** means there is no fixed table structure and 
records can have different fields. This is useful when requirements 
evolve quickly like in a startup that ships new features every week.

**Non-relational data model** means data is not organized as tables 
and joins. Instead it uses key-value pairs, JSON documents, wide 
rows or graph nodes and edges depending on the database type.

**Horizontal scalability** means you add more machines instead of 
buying a bigger one. This is the fundamental shift that made NoSQL 
attractive to companies like Google and Amazon.

**Distributed fault-tolerant design** means data is replicated 
across multiple nodes so no single failure brings everything down.

**Relaxed consistency** through BASE properties is the one that 
took me the longest to wrap my head around.

## ACID vs BASE: The Core Trade-off

I was very familiar with ACID from DBS101. Atomicity means all or 
nothing. Consistency means data is always valid. Isolation means 
transactions do not interfere with each other. Durability means 
committed data survives failures.

NoSQL systems often follow BASE instead.

The contrast between the two is straightforward. ACID systems 
prioritize **immediate and strong consistency** and are willing to 
sacrifice availability when needed making them ideal for financial 
transactions. BASE systems flip that priority by making 
**availability the highest concern** and accepting that consistency 
will arrive eventually making them ideal for social media and big 
data workloads. In terms of scaling, ACID systems grow vertically 
by upgrading to larger servers while BASE systems grow horizontally 
by adding more servers to the cluster.

BASE breaks down into three ideas. **Basically Available** means 
the system always responds to a request even if the data is not 
the most recent version. It prefers giving you *some* data over 
returning a timeout error. **Soft state** means the state of the 
system may change over time even without new input because nodes 
are constantly communicating to synchronize. **Eventually 
Consistent** is the core idea which means if you write data to 
Node A it might take a few milliseconds for Node B and Node C to 
reflect that change but they will get there.

This feels uncomfortable at first because eventually consistent 
sounds like sometimes wrong. But for things like a social media 
like count or a friend list updating across regions, a few 
milliseconds of lag is completely acceptable. The system stays 
responsive rather than locking everything until every node agrees.

## The CAP Theorem

This is the theoretical backbone of Unit 1. Eric Brewer proposed 
the CAP Theorem in 2000 and it was formally proven by Gilbert and 
Lynch in 2002. It states that any distributed system can only 
guarantee **two of the three** properties simultaneously.

```
        Consistency
             △
            / \
           /   \
          /     \
         /       \
        ▽_________▽
  Availability  Partition Tolerance
```


**Consistency** means every read receives the most recent write 
and all clients see the same data at the same time. 
**Availability** means every request receives a non-error response 
regardless of the state of any individual node so the system is 
always ready to respond. **Partition Tolerance** means the system 
continues operating even when network messages are lost or parts 
of the network fail.

Here is the key insight. **Partition Tolerance is non-negotiable** 
in any real distributed system because networks *will* fail. So in 
practice you are always choosing between Consistency and 
Availability during a partition.

CP systems prioritize Consistency over Availability meaning they 
may become unavailable during a partition rather than return stale 
data. AP systems prioritize Availability over Consistency meaning 
they return responses even if not all nodes agree and rely on 
eventual consistency to catch up.

Most NoSQL databases are AP systems which is exactly why they use 
BASE rather than ACID. Some modern systems offer **tunable 
consistency** so you can choose per-operation whether to favor 
consistency or availability depending on what that specific 
operation requires. That flexibility is genuinely powerful.

## The Six Types of NoSQL Databases

This was the most eye-opening part of Unit 1 because I realized 
that NoSQL is not one thing. There are fundamentally different 
data models and each one is suited to different problems.

## Key-Value Stores

The simplest NoSQL model. Data is stored as key-value pairs where 
the key is unique and the value is opaque to the database meaning 
the database does not know or care what is inside the value.
```
GET  user:1042
SET  user:1042  {"name": "Tenzin", "session": "abc123"}
DEL  user:1042
```

Think of it as a massive distributed hash map with persistent 
storage. Examples include Redis and Amazon DynamoDB. Best suited 
for caching, session storage, rate limiting and leaderboards. The 
limitation is that you cannot run rich queries on the contents of 
the value because only lookups by key are supported.

## Document Databases

Store data as JSON or BSON documents grouped into collections. 
Each document can have a completely different structure with no 
rigid schema enforced.
```json
{
  "_id": "post_001",
  "title": "Introduction to NoSQL",
  "tags": ["databases", "nosql"],
  "author": { "name": "Saroj", "year": 3 },
  "views": 142
}
```

Examples include MongoDB and CouchDB. Perfect for content 
management, user profiles, product catalogs and event logs which 
are all cases where structure varies per record or evolves 
frequently over time.

## Column-Family Stores

Also called wide-column stores. They look like tables on the 
surface but each row can have completely different columns and the 
storage is optimized for write-heavy workloads. The key difference 
from RDBMS is that data modelling here is **query-driven** meaning 
you design your tables around the queries you will run rather than 
around the shape of your data.

Examples include Apache Cassandra and HBase. Best for time-series 
data, IoT metrics and large-scale event logging with predictable 
query patterns.

## Graph Databases

Store data as **nodes** which represent entities and **edges** 
which represent relationships where both can have their own 
properties. They are purpose-built for traversing complex 
relationships efficiently because operations that would require 
many expensive joins in a relational database become first-class 
operations here.

Examples include Neo4j and Amazon Neptune. Best for social 
networks, fraud detection, recommendation engines and knowledge 
graphs. If your data is fundamentally about connections between 
things then graph databases win.

## Time-Series Databases

Optimized for data points indexed by timestamp such as sensor 
readings, server metrics and financial tick data. They include 
built-in features like automatic downsampling, retention policies 
and time-window aggregations that you would have to build yourself 
on top of a general-purpose database.

Examples include InfluxDB, Prometheus and TimescaleDB. The data 
pattern is always a timestamp combined with a metric name, optional 
tags and a value.

## Vector Databases

The newest category and the most relevant to modern AI workloads. 
They store high-dimensional vectors which are numerical 
representations of data called embeddings and they enable 
similarity search based on distance metrics like cosine similarity 
or Euclidean distance.

Examples include Pinecone, Milvus and Weaviate. They power 
semantic search, recommendation engines and retrieval-augmented 
generation with large language models. This is where databases 
are heading and it is worth paying attention to early.

## How to Choose the Right NoSQL Database

The module framed selection around four criteria and I found this 
genuinely useful for thinking through design problems.

**Data structure and access patterns** is the most important 
starting point. If you only need key-based lookups then a 
key-value store is the answer. If you need nested documents with 
flexible schema and field-based queries then a document store 
fits. If you have large-scale append-only metrics with predictable 
queries then column-family or time-series is right. If your data 
is fundamentally about relationships and traversals then graph 
is the answer. If you need ML-based similarity search then vector 
is the choice.

**Scalability requirements** ask how much data you expect, what 
the read-to-write ratio looks like and whether you need 
single-region or global distribution. High write throughput at 
global scale points toward column-family systems like Cassandra. 
Read-heavy workloads benefit from a key-value cache in front of 
any primary store. Multi-region with eventual consistency 
acceptable points toward AP systems.

**Consistency needs** depend entirely on the domain. Banking, 
inventory and financial systems need strong consistency and point 
toward CP systems. Social feeds, like counts and view counters 
can tolerate eventual consistency and work well with AP systems. 
Many modern NoSQL systems offer tunable consistency so you can 
decide per-operation.

**Ecosystem and tooling** is a practical reality. Sometimes the 
technically better choice loses to the one with better language 
drivers, managed cloud services, monitoring tools and community 
support. Operational considerations matter as much as theoretical 
ones.

## The DrukRide Activity

The activity for this unit involved designing the data layer for 
**DrukRide**, a fictional Bhutan-wide ride-hailing platform similar 
to Uber. It forced me to actually apply everything above rather 
than just recite definitions.

The key insight the activity gave me is that no single database 
handles everything well. The answer is **polyglot persistence** 
which means using the right database for each data domain.

The DrukRide backend uses a different database for each concern. 
**PostgreSQL** handles trip lifecycle and payments because ACID 
guarantees are non-negotiable since no passenger can be charged 
twice or matched with two drivers for one request. **Redis** 
manages real-time driver locations and sessions because it 
delivers millisecond reads with built-in TTL-based expiry for 
stale location data. **MongoDB** stores user and driver profiles 
because the schema is flexible with varying KYC fields and the 
workload is mostly reads after initial sign-up. **Cassandra** 
handles analytics and trip history logs because it is built for 
write-heavy time-based query patterns at scale. **Neo4j** powers 
driver-passenger preference matching because that problem is 
fundamentally about traversing relationship history efficiently.

The most interesting part was working through the CAP decisions. 
For trip booking you must choose CP because a passenger cannot be 
charged twice or matched with two drivers. The system should return 
an error during a partition rather than allow that inconsistency. 
For the live driver map you choose AP because a slightly stale 
location is perfectly fine and the map should keep working even 
if the network link between Thimphu and Phuentsholing is degraded. 
For the analytics dashboard you also choose AP because management 
seeing trip counts that are five minutes old is completely 
acceptable compared to the dashboard going offline entirely.

## What I Am Taking Away From Unit 1

Three weeks ago I thought "database" meant relational database. 
Now I understand that is like saying "vehicle" means only cars.

The biggest mental shift was around consistency. I used to see 
ACID as the only correct approach. Unit 1 showed me that eventual 
consistency is not a compromise but rather a deliberate and 
rational design choice that enables systems to serve billions of 
users at low latency. The CAP theorem makes the trade-off explicit 
and forces you to think about what your system actually needs 
rather than defaulting to whatever feels safest.

The second shift was around schema design. In RDBMS you normalize 
first and write queries to join the pieces back together. In 
column-family stores you design your schema around your queries 
first. That is a fundamentally different way of thinking and it 
does not come naturally after years of relational thinking.

The third thing I am carrying forward is the polyglot persistence 
mindset. Real production systems at scale almost never use a 
single database. Understanding when and why to reach for each 
type of database is one of the most practical skills this module 
is building toward.

curious to see how the theory maps to actual query patterns and 
real data modelling decisions.
